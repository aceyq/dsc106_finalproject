Climate Futures Explorer

DSC 106 Final Project – Initial Prototype

Project Overview

This prototype explores how projected changes in global temperature and precipitation under different CMIP6 scenarios may shape future climate patterns and global vegetation. Users can compare emission pathways and regional outcomes using the controls below.

The current version focuses on two core time series visualizations. Future iterations will add narrative annotations, maps, and explicit links to vegetation and biome changes.

Project Write-Up

1. What have you done so far?

So far, we have built the core structure of the Climate Futures Explorer prototype, including the user interface, dropdown controls, and two main D3 time-series visualizations. We have connected the charts to scenario and region selectors so that the user can dynamically explore differences in future climate projections. We also integrated processed temperature and precipitation datasets from the CMIP6 archive and ensured they load efficiently through JavaScript. Additionally, we created a cohesive design system with consistent styling, padded card components, and a dark background theme to increase readability and focus.

Beyond the visuals, we have outlined the narrative flow that the final project will follow, including how temperature trends, precipitation shifts, and ecological responses will be introduced in a step-by-step story. We set up the structure for adding future annotations and explanations to guide users toward important insights. Overall, the prototype now includes all foundational elements needed for the explorable explanation we plan to build.

We have also begun experimenting with alternative visualization ideas, including preliminary map mockups and concept sketches for a linked-view interface that connects spatial patterns to the time-series trends already implemented. This early design exploration helps us evaluate which visual forms best support the story we want to tell and where future improvements should be targeted. In parallel, we organized our data-processing workflow so new CMIP6 variables or vegetation indicators can be incorporated seamlessly later. By establishing a flexible codebase and clear visual direction at this stage, we are preparing the foundation needed to scale the project into a richer explorable explanation.

2. What will be the most challenging part of the project and why?

The most challenging part of the project will be designing an integrated narrative that smoothly connects the climate variables to ecological outcomes while keeping the interface intuitive and uncluttered. Climate data is inherently complex, and adding meaningful explanations without overwhelming users requires careful information design. Another major difficulty will be integrating spatial data, such as maps or vegetation classifications, into the existing time-series framework. Ensuring that the visual transitions feel coherent and that users understand how each chart relates to the evolving story will take significant iteration.

We also anticipate challenges in balancing interactivity with interpretability. As we add more layers—scenarios, regions, biome indicators, and narrative steps—it becomes harder to maintain visual clarity and avoid cognitive overload. Developing a layout that supports multiple linked views while remaining visually clean will require thoughtful prototyping and user-first design decisions. Ultimately, the biggest challenge is not technical, but ensuring the final narrative remains compelling, accessible, and scientifically accurate.

Another possible challenge will be communicating uncertainty responsibly without overwhelming users with technical details. CMIP6 models contain variability across institutions, scenarios, and ensemble members, and presenting this uncertainty transparently, whether through error bands, model spreads, or qualitative explanations, requires careful judgment. We want viewers to understand both the robustness of long-term climate trends and the nuance in regional projections. Finding the right level of detail and the right visual encodings to convey uncertainty will be essential to maintaining scientific accuracy while still providing a smooth, engaging user experience.

How Different Climate Futures Diverge

Scroll through the story beats below. Each step highlights a different CMIP6 emissions pathway. As you move down the page, the visualizations on the right will focus on that scenario, while the region dropdown lets you see how the same pathway plays out in different parts of the world.

SSP1-2.6 – Rapid mitigation

In a world that aggressively cuts emissions, global temperature still rises, but the curve stays relatively shallow. Precipitation changes are present, yet less extreme, giving ecosystems more time to adapt before major biome shifts are locked in.

SSP2-4.5 – “Middle-of-the-road” pathway

This pathway is close to our current trajectory. Warming accelerates after mid-century, and precipitation shifts become more noticeable—more intense rainfall in some regions and drying in others—raising pressure on crops, forests, and water systems.

SSP3-7.0 – High emissions, uneven action

In this scenario, global cooperation stalls. Temperatures climb much more steeply, and rainfall patterns become more volatile. Some regions face repeated droughts while others see heavier downpours, increasing the risk of vegetation stress and biome disruption.

SSP5-8.5 – Fossil-fuel intensive future

This fossil-fuel heavy world leads to the fastest warming and the largest shifts in precipitation. It represents a “worst-case” benchmark where ecosystems have the least time to adapt, and the likelihood of large-scale biome transitions is highest.

Explore Scenarios and Regions

Use the dropdown to choose a world region. Use the buttons to select which scenario(s). Both charts below will update to reflect your selection. The narrative on the left highlights one scenario at a time to guide your attention.

Projected Temperature Change

This chart shows projected near-surface air temperature (°C) for the selected scenario and region from 1850 to 2100 (where available). Higher-emissions scenarios typically lead to steeper warming.

Projected Precipitation Change

This chart shows projected daily precipitation (mm/day) for the same scenario and region. Changes in rainfall patterns can contribute to vegetation greening, browning, or biome shifts.

Planned Narrative Structure

In the final project, we will extend this prototype into a full explorable explanation that:

  • Introduces global temperature trajectories across CMIP6 emission pathways.
  • Highlights regional contrasts in temperature and precipitation change.
  • Connects climate projections to potential vegetation responses and biome transitions.
